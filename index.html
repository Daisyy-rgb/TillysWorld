The World

Spring of the 993rd Free Year (993 A.G.)

History

Long ago, God controlled the vast majority of magic and used it to set predetermined fates for all living things. The King of Humanity, the Fairy Paragon, and the largest Fragment of God rebelled and killed him. This freed magic for everyone — but chaos followed.

The three rebels reconvened and imposed limits on magic through an agreement known as The Binding. The world is set in a medieval-early renaissance era. The Free Calendar counts from the death of God.

The Mother Goddess

The Mother Goddess predates God entirely. Fairies are her children. She allied with the King of Humanity and the Fairy Paragon to defeat God, but spent everything in the process and fell into a deep sleep.

As part of their agreement she still sends her essence into the world while sleeping — this manifests as Fairy Fountains, which appear randomly. Anything submerged in a Fairy Fountain gains magical properties. They are essentially her dreams bleeding through into the waking world.

Most humans don't know she exists. Even most Fairies have forgotten her. The wealthy hoard Fairy Fountains, draining them for personal use. Finding one before the rich claim it is nearly impossible.

Notable Locations

Alderrdeen Township

The city where the story begins. Cobblestone streets, candlelight, merchant stalls, nobles who hoard Fairy Fountains. Home to Eustace's Oddities.

Magic System

Magic directs — it does not create

The Principle

Magic directs — it does not create. You cannot conjure something from nothing. You redirect forces that already exist in the world.

Magic is performed by drawing sigil circles. Each sigil represents a fundamental force. Combinations of sigils create instructions. To make fire from nothing requires combining Air, Motion/Force, Heat, Direction, and Containment sigils — five sigils combined correctly just to produce something resembling a fireball.

Regular magic takes years of practice to master. Big spells can take weeks to prepare. Magic is used mostly for small daily tasks.

The Binding & Forbidden Magic

After God's death, the three rebels imposed The Binding. Forbidden magic draws from the raw power released when God fell — faster, stronger, no years of practice required.

The moment someone uses forbidden magic, every Fairy in the vicinity feels it. They come. They punish — killing some, turning others into monsters. No mercy. No context. No exceptions.

Forbidden mages have survived by studying Fairy behavior for centuries. They have mapped territories, travel speeds, and patterns. They also hide behind fragments of the Skeleton Key, which drowns out the signal of their magic entirely.

Arcanists

Magic users who have chosen a Sovereignty — a discipline that shapes both their abilities and their soul over time — are called Arcanists. Each Sovereignty has 10 Steps plus a Step 0, numbered from Step 0 to Step X (nearly unreachable pinnacle). Sanctioned practitioners change slowly. Forbidden magic practitioners change much faster and far more dangerously.

Fairies & Towers

Children of the Mother Goddess

About Fairies

Fairies are the Mother Goddess's children — biologically identical regardless of type, but culturally divided. They can only use large amounts of magic when channeling it through a human.

Fairies are deeply racist toward one another despite being biologically the same. Many have forgotten the Mother Goddess. Fairies manifest in different forms when enforcing The Binding — some as storms, some as humans, others as shapeless monsters.

Types of Fairies

Djinn

Reside in Fairy Towers. Anyone who clears a tower to the top claims the Djinn's treasure and a piece of their power, usually as a Living Artifact.

Daemons

Offer power through deals, taking body parts, memories, emotions, firstborn children, or other personal costs in exchange.

Angels

Give power only to their most devout followers.

Others (In Development)

Asura, Devas, Nymphs, Spirits, Wraiths, Shades. Each gives magic away differently. Some are malicious.

Fairy Towers

Enormously difficult to clear. Even kings wouldn't attempt them — the cost in lives would be catastrophic. This makes them the great equalizer: no amount of wealth or power substitutes for the skill and luck required to reach the top alone. Each tower is different. Each Djinn is different. The Living Artifact rewarded varies by tower.

Living Artifacts

Objects that contain a Fairy and hold one spell or power

Living Artifacts contain a Fairy and hold one spell or power within them. Clearing a Fairy Tower earns one. They are extraordinarily rare and valuable.

Eustace's Sword

Contained a fire Djinn. Every swing released flame. Eustace earned it in his youth clearing a tower. He kept it secret for decades. He spent it protecting Tilly at the end of Chapter I.

The Skeleton Key

The finger of the King of Humanity, who threw himself into a Fairy Fountain and made every part of his body holy. Allows travel through unseen doorways — any door leads to any other door, unpredictably. The longer it is used the more it infects the user's body. Fused to Tilly's right hand.

Deeper Significance

Each piece of the King's body resonates loudly with Fairies — not as a threat, but as something sacred. This has an unintended effect: it drowns out the signal of forbidden magic being used nearby. Whoever holds a fragment can use forbidden magic without Fairy detection.

The Kingdom holds half the fragments (believing them simply powerful relics). Forbidden mages hold the other half (knowing exactly what they do). Tilly holds one piece neither side has acquired. She is now caught between two forces who want her — for different reasons, neither of which they will tell her plainly.

Chapters

Spring of the 993rd Free Year

Chapter I
The Skeleton Key

Tilly works the counter at Eustace's Oddities. A worn-down adventurer comes in looking at swords. She reads him immediately — no money, genuine need — and sells him one for practically nothing. Then a noble walks in. Same product. She sells it for ten times the price without blinking.

She visits Eustace, who is bedridden. He coughs. She decides to find a Fairy Fountain to enchant his medicine and heal him. She searches the outskirts of Alderrdeen all day. Beautiful landscapes. No fountain. The wealthy have claimed them all.

Returning home hungry, she steals bread from a market stall. Guards give chase. She rounds a corner into an alley and nearly steps on a dying man. He begs her not to give him away. She throws a tarp over him and acts casual.

Two guard groups converge — one chasing a bread thief, one hunting someone who escaped with a Living Artifact. The second group waves the first away. Tilly is alone with the dying man.

He presses something into her hand — a long pale bone-like key. It fuses to her finger before she can react. He tells her to run. It doesn't matter where. Just don't let it fall into the wrong hands. Then he is gone.

Tilly returns home. She has only moments with Eustace before guards arrive demanding the artifact. It won't leave her hand. They decide to take the finger. Eustace rises from his bed, unwraps his Living Artifact, and the sword erupts in flame.

"You will do no such thing."

He holds them off. Tilly is pushed through the back door. Eustace throws his sword through just before it closes. The guards who follow find themselves in the back alleyway. Tilly finds herself somewhere else entirely.

A snowfield. Silent. Cold. No city. No guards. No grandfather. Just Tilly, alone, with a dying flaming sword in the snow and a dead king's finger fused to her hand.

This is when she becomes a Candlebearer.
